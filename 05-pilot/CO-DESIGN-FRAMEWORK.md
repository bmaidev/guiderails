# Co-design framework

**Status:** v0.1, 9 July 2026. Applies to the standard, the reference implementation, the benchmark, and any pilot.

---

## 1. Position

This project's central claim is that agents are assistive technology serving people with disability first. A claim of that kind made *about* people with disability without being made *with* them is worthless — and would be identified as such within minutes by the sector it addresses. Co-design here is a structural feature with decision authority, budget, and an audit trail, not a consultation activity.

## 2. Principles

1. **Nothing about us without us.** People with disability shape what is built, how it is tested, and what is claimed about it.
2. **Co-design is not consultation.** Consultation asks for reactions to decisions; co-design shares the decisions. This framework names which decisions are shared (§3).
3. **Lived experience is expertise and is paid as expertise.** Participants are remunerated at professional advisory rates under a published remuneration policy set in the delivery budget. No unpaid participation, including "feedback sessions".
4. **The process is accessible by default.** Auslan interpretation, live captioning, Easy Read materials for every decision artefact, screen-reader-tested collateral, breaks and pacing, and asynchronous participation options — provisioned without being asked.
5. **Trauma-informed.** Some participants will have adverse histories with the very services under discussion, including compliance and payment-suspension experiences. Facilitation, consent and support practices are designed for that reality.
6. **Intersectional by design.** Disability intersects with First Nations identity, cultural and linguistic diversity, age, and regional access. Panel composition targets reflect it (§4).
7. **Closed loops.** Every input receives a documented response: adopted, adapted, or declined with reasons ("you said, we did / we didn't, because").
8. **Privacy and safety.** Data minimisation, de-identification by default, participant control over attribution, and a published complaints path independent of the facilitation team.

## 3. Governance — the Lived Experience Design Authority (LEDA)

A standing panel of 6–10 people with disability (composition per §4), supported but not chaired by the steward.

**LEDA decides (consent gates — the work does not proceed without its consent):**
- **C1.** Every public claim about benefit to people with disability, before publication.
- **C2.** The benchmark task suite's realism and its disability-relevant scenarios, before benchmark runs are scored for publication.
- **C3.** Easy Read and plain-language versions of the standard's public artefacts.
- **C4.** Changes to this framework itself.

**LEDA advises (steward decides, dissent published):**
- Success-criterion wording and level assignments; prioritisation between guidelines; pilot design choices; reference-implementation UX.

**Mechanics.** Consent at a meeting requires the agreement of a majority of members present, with at least three members present. Where LEDA withholds consent, the item returns with changes or is dropped; the steward MUST NOT publish over a withheld consent gate. Dissents and minority views on advisory items are published with the artefact. Decisions are logged in DECISIONS.md with LEDA as owner for C1–C4 items.

**Why real authority:** a body that only advises would fail this project's own governance test — remove it and nothing changes. LEDA is constructed so that removing it visibly breaks the pipeline to publication.

## 4. Composition and recruitment

**Targets (panel-level, not per-person quotas):** cross-disability representation spanning intellectual disability, psychosocial disability, blind/low vision, Deaf/hard of hearing, physical/motor disability, and chronic illness/fatigue conditions; at least one First Nations member; CALD representation; regional/remote representation; current or recent lived experience of high-compliance service systems; assistive-technology expertise (at least one member who is an AT power user or trainer).

**Recruitment** is through disability representative organisations, approached as partners with their own funded roles, not as free recruitment channels. Candidate partner categories (to be approached; none yet engaged): national cross-disability peaks and advocacy bodies (e.g. AFDO, PWDA), First Peoples Disability Network, National Ethnic Disability Alliance, cohort peaks (e.g. Inclusion Australia, Blind Citizens Australia, Deaf Australia), and assistive-technology sector bodies (e.g. ATSA). Final partner list is a LEDA-advised, steward-decided item.

**Supporters.** Members may participate with a supporter of their choosing; supporters are also remunerated for session time.

## 5. Method by phase

**Phase 1 (standard v0.2 → v0.9):**
- Principle and criterion validation workshops using scenario walkthroughs ("Amina reports her fortnight using her agent; where does this break for her?") rather than specification review.
- Easy Read co-production of the five principles and the conformance model (gate C3).
- Terminology testing for the glossary discipline in 2.3 — the words the standard makes services use should be words users recognise.

**Phase 2 (reference implementation and benchmark):**
- Task-suite co-design: LEDA members and recruited participants define what realistic task briefs look like for the fixture journeys, including the recovery, ineligibility and interruption cases (gate C2).
- Paired sessions: an AT user and an agent attempt the same journey on both builds; think-aloud protocol; findings feed both the fixture and the failure taxonomy.
- Optional human-participant usability arm of the benchmark (see BENCHMARK-METHODOLOGY §9) — proceeds only under the ethics decision at §6.

**Phase 3 (public exposure):** LEDA review of the public v0.9 pack (gate C1 on all benefit claims); accessible submission channels for the public comment period, so the comment process itself conforms to the project's principles.

**Pilot (any delivery program):** LEDA representation in pilot governance with the same consent-gate pattern, funded within the pilot budget.

## 6. Ethics and safeguarding

- Informed consent per activity; plain-language and Easy Read consent materials; right to withdraw at any time including retrospective withdrawal of attributable contributions.
- Distress protocol for sessions touching compliance histories, with warm referral pathways and facilitator training.
- **Open decision (OD-06):** whether benchmark human-arm results intended for research publication require HREC review. Default posture: if we will publish it as research, we seek review.
- Incident and complaints path published; complaints about the process go to a named contact independent of session facilitators.

## 7. Quality measures (published each phase)

- Composition against §4 targets.
- Decision-influence log: count and list of artefact changes attributable to co-design input, and declined items with reasons.
- Participant experience: post-activity ratings gathered accessibly; results published with responses.
- Payment integrity: sessions paid within stated terms; exceptions reported.

## 8. Budget note

Co-design is a first-class budget line in any delivery program — covering remuneration, partner-organisation funding, accessibility provisioning, facilitation, and safeguarding — and is never the contingency that gets cut. Rates and totals are set in the delivery budget, not in this framework.
